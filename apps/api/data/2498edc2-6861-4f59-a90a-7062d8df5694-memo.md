## 3. Precedent analysis

Hyperliquid's trajectory parallels dYdX's evolution from an L2 application to a standalone appchain (dYdX v4 on Cosmos). Both protocols bet that derivatives trading requires a purpose-built chain rather than a general-purpose L1. dYdX made this transition in 2023 and saw initial TVL growth followed by stagnation as the token unlock schedule pressured price. The key difference: Hyperliquid launched as an L1 from day one rather than migrating, avoiding the disruption cost.

A closer comparison is BNB Chain and Binance. Hyperliquid's L1 functions as a vertically integrated exchange chain, similar to how BSC served Binance's DeFi ambitions. Both feature centralized teams running a high-performance chain optimized for trading. Binance's model produced substantial value for BNB holders despite the absence of meaningful governance.

The GMX parallel is also instructive. GMX built a derivatives protocol on Arbitrum and achieved significant TVL, but struggled with fee distribution debates and governance concentration. GMX generated roughly $100M in annualized fees at peak. Hyperliquid generates over 7x that figure at $762.7M annualized [DefiLlama, 2026-03-08], with a simpler organizational structure.

## 6. Governance analysis

### Protocol structure

Hyperliquid is team-driven. There is no Snapshot space, no governance forum, and no onchain voting mechanism [Snapshot, 2026-03-08]. Protocol decisions are made by the Hyperliquid team and foundation.

### Design choice, not deficiency

This memo treats the absence of governance as a deliberate design choice. Many of the most successful technology companies and financial infrastructure providers operate without shareholder governance during their growth phases. Coinbase, for example, went public with a dual-class share structure that concentrated control with management. Hyperliquid's team-driven model is functionally equivalent.

### Implications for this analysis

Because governance does not exist, the fund's activism playbook does not apply. Our position thesis rests on value and growth, not on governance participation or coalition building. If governance launches in the future, it could create an additional catalyst for engagement, but it is not required for the investment case.

## 9. Technical architecture

Hyperliquid runs a custom L1 consensus chain optimized for order book operations. The chain achieves sub-second block latency and processes up to 100,000 orders per second. This performance comes from a purpose-built architecture rather than a general-purpose VM.

HyperEVM provides Ethereum Virtual Machine compatibility as a separate execution environment on the same chain. This allows third-party developers to deploy Solidity contracts while the core exchange logic runs natively. The dual-layer design separates high-performance trading from general-purpose smart contract execution.

Audit history is not available through public channels. The closed-source nature of the core chain code means external security review is limited to the HyperEVM layer. The March 2025 JELLY vault incident demonstrated that the system is not immune to exploit vectors, particularly around vault mechanics and price manipulation. Smart contract risk assessment depends on the team's internal security practices, which are not publicly documented.

## 10. Risk assessment

Dilution Risk: Critical. 76.2% of HYPE's max supply is not yet circulating [CoinGecko, 2026-03-08]. Vesting schedules and unlock dates are not publicly available. At current prices, each 10% tranche of new supply entering circulation represents roughly $3B in potential sell pressure. This is the single largest risk to the position.

Regulatory Risk: High. Hyperliquid operates as a derivatives exchange, which attracts regulatory attention globally. No clear jurisdiction has been established. The token could face securities classification scrutiny given the team's control over the protocol and the staking mechanisms. Derivatives exchanges are under increasing regulatory pressure across the US, EU, and Asia.

Smart Contract Risk: Tier 2 (Medium). Custom L1 with closed-source core code. No public audit history. The March 2025 JELLY incident demonstrated real vulnerability in vault mechanics. The proprietary codebase means the security community cannot independently verify the system's integrity.

Market Risk: High. HYPE is down 48.2% from its ATH of $59.30 [CoinGecko, 2026-03-08]. The token has high beta to broader crypto markets. A sustained crypto bear market would compress both trading volume and fees, directly impacting the revenue thesis.

Team Key-Person Risk: Medium. The protocol is entirely team-dependent with no community governance backstop. If key team members leave or make poor strategic decisions, there is no formal mechanism for course correction.
